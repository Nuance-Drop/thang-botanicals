<div style="max-width: 800px; margin: 0 auto; padding: 2rem;">
  <h1 style="font-family: 'Cinzel', serif; color: #D4AF37;">Traditional Use in Thai Culture: Quid Chewing Rituals</h1>
  
  <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); font-size: 1.1rem; line-height: 1.8; margin: 2rem 0;">
    In Southern Thailand's <em>biak-biak</em> culture, kratom was never just a drug—it was a <strong>social institution</strong>. Understanding these traditions reveals how prohibition disrupted centuries of harm-reduction practice.
  </p>

  <h2 style="color: #D4AF37; font-family: 'Cinzel'; margin-top: 3rem;">The Quid: Preparation and Ritual</h2>
  
  <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 1.8;">
    Traditional use involved <strong>fresh leaves</strong>, not dried powder. Users—predominantly Muslim men in Pattani, Yala, and Narathiwat provinces—would:
  </p>

  <ol style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 2;">
    <li>Pick 10-20 fresh leaves from wild trees (never plantations)</li>
    <li>Wash and de-stem the leaves</li>
    <li>Roll into a <em>quid</em> (ball) with salt or dried shrimp paste</li>
    <li>Chew slowly over 30-60 minutes, swallowing juices</li>
    <li>Spit out fibrous remains</li>
  </ol>

  <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 1.8;">
    This method delivers <strong>lower, sustained alkaloid absorption</strong> than modern "toss and wash"—reducing overdose risk.
  </p>

  <h2 style="color: #D4AF37; font-family: 'Cinzel'; margin-top: 3rem;">Social Functions</h2>
  
  <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 1.8;">
    Kratom chewing occurred in <strong>community gatherings</strong> called <em>majlis</em>, similar to coffee houses:
  </p>

  <ul style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 2;">
    <li><strong>Work coordination:</strong> Farmers planning harvests</li>
    <li><strong>Dispute resolution:</strong> Village elders mediating conflicts</li>
    <li><strong>Religious study:</strong> Quran recitation groups</li>
    <li><strong>Opium alternative:</strong> Harm reduction for those avoiding addiction</li>
  </ul>

  <div style="background: rgba(255,0,110,0.1); border: 1px solid rgba(255,0,110,0.3); padding: 1.5rem; border-radius: 8px; margin: 2rem 0;">
    <strong style="color: #ff006e;">The 1943 Ban and Cultural Disruption</strong>
    <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); margin-top: 0.5rem;">
      The Kratom Act 2486 was passed not for public health, but because kratom competed with <strong>state opium monopoly revenue</strong>. The law destroyed traditional social structures without reducing use—simply driving it underground.
    </p>
  </div>

  <h2 style="color: #D4AF37; font-family: 'Cinzel'; margin-top: 3rem;">Modern Revival</h2>
  
  <p style="color: rgba(255,255,255,0.9); line-height: 1.8;">
    Since Thailand decriminalized kratom in 2022, traditional <em>quid</em> preparation is experiencing a renaissance. Community gardens in Pattani now teach younger generations the pre-prohibition methods—preserving a harm-reduction tradition older than the nation-state itself.
  </p>

  </p>
